## 7. Standing Wave Modes (Detailed Analysis)

#### **1. System Parameters and Unit Conversion**
To maintain consistency with SI units, we first convert the physical dimensions of the system:
* **Length of the string ($L$):** 80 cm = 0.80 m
* **Number of antinodes ($n$):** 4
* **Boundary Conditions:** We assume the string is fixed at both ends (nodes at $x=0$ and $x=L$).

#### **2. Physical Principles of Standing Waves**
A standing wave is formed by the interference of two traveling waves moving in opposite directions. On a string fixed at both ends, the distance between two consecutive nodes (points of zero displacement) is exactly half of one wavelength ($\lambda/2$). 

The regions of maximum displacement between these nodes are called **antinodes**. Therefore:
* 1 Antinode = $\frac{1}{2}$ Wavelength
* 2 Antinodes = 1 Full Wavelength

#### **3. Establishing the Relationship Between $L$ and $\lambda$**
In any harmonic $n$, the total length of the string $L$ must accommodate an integer number of half-wavelengths. This is expressed by the formula:
$$L = n \left( \frac{\lambda}{2} \right)$$

Given that we have 4 antinodes ($n=4$), we substitute this into our relationship:
$$L = 4 \left( \frac{\lambda}{2} \right)$$
$$L = 2\lambda$$

#### **4. Algebraic Solution for Wavelength ($\lambda$)**
We now isolate $\lambda$ to solve for the specific wavelength produced in this fourth harmonic mode:
$$\lambda = \frac{L}{2}$$

Substituting the known length of the string:
$$\lambda = \frac{0.80 \text{ m}}{2}$$

**Final Result:**
**$\lambda = 0.40 \text{ m}$** *(or 40 cm)*

*(Conclusion: In the fourth harmonic mode on an 80 cm string, the wavelength is exactly half the length of the string, resulting in two full wave cycles fitting within the boundaries).*
