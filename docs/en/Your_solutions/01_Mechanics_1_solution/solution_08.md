# Circular Motion: Centripetal Acceleration on Earth's Equator

This document calculates the centripetal acceleration experienced by an object or person standing on the Earth's equator due to its rotation.

---

## 1. Given Parameters
* **Earth's Radius ($R$):** $6378 \text{ km} = 6,378,000 \text{ m}$
* **Rotation Period ($T$):** Approximately 24 hours (1 solar day)
    * $T = 24 \times 3600 \text{ s} = 86,400 \text{ s}$

---

## 2. Calculating Angular Velocity ($\omega$)
The angular velocity of the Earth is given by the formula:
$$\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T}$$

Substituting the rotation period:
$$\omega = \frac{2\pi}{86,400} \approx 7.272 \times 10^{-5} \text{ rad/s}$$

---

## 3. Calculating Centripetal Acceleration ($a_c$)
The formula for centripetal acceleration is:
$$a_c = \omega^2 R$$

Substituting our values:
$$a_c = (7.272 \times 10^{-5} \text{ rad/s})^2 \times 6,378,000 \text{ m}$$
$$a_c = 5.288 \times 10^{-9} \times 6,378,000$$
$$a_c \approx 0.0337 \text{ m/s}^2$$

---

## 4. Final Result
The centripetal acceleration of a person at the Earth's equator is approximately:
$$\mathbf{0.0337 \text{ m/s}^2}$$

### Comparison
To put this in perspective, the acceleration due to gravity ($g$) is approximately $9.81 \text{ m/s}^2$. The centripetal acceleration at the equator is only about **0.34%** of the force of gravity, which is why we don't feel like we're being "flung off" the planet.
