# Problem 10: Magnetic Force on a Current-Carrying Wire

### 1. Physical Principle
The magnetic force ($\vec{F}$) exerted on a straight wire of length $L$ carrying a current $I$ in a uniform magnetic field $B$ is given by the formula:
$$\vec{F} = I (\vec{L} \times \vec{B})$$

The magnitude of this force depends on the angle $\theta$ between the direction of the current and the magnetic field lines:
$$F = I L B \sin(\theta)$$

### 2. Given Data
* Length of the wire ($L$): $2.0 \text{ m}$
* Current ($I$): $10 \text{ A}$
* Magnetic Field ($B$): $0.5 \text{ T}$

First, we calculate the constant product of $I$, $L$, and $B$:
$$I \times L \times B = (10 \text{ A}) \times (2.0 \text{ m}) \times (0.5 \text{ T}) = 10 \text{ N}$$

---

### 3. Calculations for Different Angles

#### Case a) $\theta = 90^\circ$ (Perpendicular)
When the wire is perpendicular to the field, the sine function reaches its maximum value.
$$F = 10 \times \sin(90^\circ)$$
$$F = 10 \times 1 = 10 \text{ N}$$

#### Case b) $\theta = 45^\circ$
When the wire is at an angle, only the component of the magnetic field perpendicular to the wire contributes to the force.
$$F = 10 \times \sin(45^\circ)$$
$$F = 10 \times \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \approx 10 \times 0.707$$
$$F \approx 7.07 \text{ N}$$

#### Case c) $\theta = 0^\circ$ (Parallel)
When the wire is parallel to the magnetic field lines, no magnetic force is produced.
$$F = 10 \times \sin(0^\circ)$$
$$F = 10 \times 0 = 0 \text{ N}$$

---

### 4. Conclusion
The magnetic force is highly dependent on orientation. It is maximized at **$10 \text{ N}$** when perpendicular, reduced to **$7.07 \text{ N}$** at a $45^\circ$ angle, and completely vanishes (**$0 \text{ N}$**) when the wire is aligned with the field.
