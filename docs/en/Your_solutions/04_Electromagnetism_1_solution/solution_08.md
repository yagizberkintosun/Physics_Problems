# Problem 8: Magnitude of the Lorentz Force

### 1. Understanding the Lorentz Force
The Lorentz force ($\vec{F}$) describes the force exerted on a charged particle moving through magnetic and electric fields. Since there is no mentioned electric field ($E = 0$), the formula simplifies to the magnetic component:
$$\vec{F} = q(\vec{v} \times \vec{B})$$

### 2. Magnitude Calculation
The magnitude of this force is given by:
$$F = q v B \sin(\theta)$$

**Given Values:**
* Charge ($q$): $2 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$
* Velocity ($v$): $10^6 \text{ m/s}$
* Magnetic Field ($B$): $0.5 \text{ T}$
* Angle ($\theta$): $90^\circ$ (since the particle enters perpendicularly)
* Mass ($m$): $4 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg}$ (Note: Mass is not required for force magnitude)

Since $\sin(90^\circ) = 1$, the equation becomes:
$$F = q v B$$

---

### 3. Step-by-Step Calculation
Substitute the scientific notation values into the formula:
$$F = (2 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}) \times (10^6 \text{ m/s}) \times (0.5 \text{ T})$$

First, multiply the coefficients:
$$2 \times 0.5 = 1.0$$

Next, combine the powers of ten:
$$10^{-19} \times 10^6 = 10^{-13}$$

Combining these gives:
**$F = 1 \times 10^{-13} \text{ N}$**

---

### 4. Conclusion
The magnitude of the Lorentz force acting on the particle is exactly **$10^{-13} \text{ Newtons}$**. 

**Note on Dynamics:** While the mass was provided in the prompt, it only affects the *acceleration* ($a = F/m$) and the resulting radius of curvature, not the instantaneous magnitude of the magnetic force itself.
